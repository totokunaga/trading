## Dow Theory

### The Definition

Dow Theory is one of the oldest frameworks in technical analysis, developed from the writings of Charles Dow (founder of the Wall Street Journal) in the late 1800s and formalized after his death. It's less a trading strategy and more a **theory of how markets move** — the foundation that later gave rise to trend following, Elliott Wave, and much of modern technical analysis.

The theory rests on six core tenets:

**1. The market discounts everything**
Price already reflects all known information — earnings, sentiment, geopolitics, insider knowledge. This predates and parallels the Efficient Market Hypothesis.

**2. There are three types of trends**
- *Primary trend* — the major bull or bear market, lasting months to years
- *Secondary trend* — corrections against the primary trend, lasting weeks to months (typically retracing 33–66% of the prior move)
- *Minor trend* — day-to-day noise, considered largely irrelevant

**3. Primary trends have three phases**
- *Bull market*: Accumulation → Public participation → Excess/euphoria
- *Bear market*: Distribution → Public panic → Despair/capitulation

**4. Averages must confirm each other**
Dow used the Industrials and the Railroads (now Transports). A new high or low in one average only carries weight if the other *confirms* it. Divergence between them is a warning sign.

**5. Volume must confirm the trend**
Volume should expand in the direction of the primary trend and contract on counter-trend moves. Rising price on falling volume is suspicious.

**6. A trend remains in effect until a clear reversal signal**
The burden of proof is on the reversal. You assume the trend continues until the market definitively proves otherwise.

### The Popular Trading Strategy: Trend Confirmation via Higher Highs/Lower Lows

The most common practical application of Dow Theory is using **price structure to define and trade the trend** — specifically the sequence of swing highs and lows.

**Uptrend structure:** Higher highs (HH) and higher lows (HL)
**Downtrend structure:** Lower highs (LH) and lower lows (LL)

#### The Core Rules

- **Trend is intact** as long as price keeps making HH + HL (or LH + LL)
- **Trend is weakening** when a new high/low fails to exceed the prior one (a "lower high" in an uptrend is the first warning)
- **Trend is broken** when price violates the last significant swing low in an uptrend (or swing high in a downtrend) — this is the Dow Theory reversal signal

#### The Entry Approach

Rather than buying breakouts, most traders use Dow Theory to **enter on the pullback to a higher low:**

1. Confirm an uptrend is in place (at least one HH + HL sequence)
2. Price makes a new high, then pulls back
3. Watch for the pullback to hold *above* the prior swing low (confirming HL structure)
4. Enter long when price resumes upward — either at the HL or on the break of the prior high
5. Stop goes below the HL — a break there would violate the trend structure
6. Hold until the trend shows signs of reversal (a lower high forms, or the last HL is breached)

#### The Confirmation Filter

Many traders add the **dual-average confirmation rule** in modern form — for example, requiring both the S&P 500 and the Dow Transports to confirm a new high before trusting a bull trend signal. Divergence (one makes a new high, the other doesn't) is treated as a caution flag, not necessarily a sell signal on its own.

---

### Why It Endures

Dow Theory works because it's built on observable market behavior rather than indicators derived from price. It forces traders to **let the market define the trend** rather than imposing their own bias. The weakness is that reversal signals come late by design — you'll never catch the exact top or bottom, but you trade with structural confirmation rather than against it.

It pairs naturally with horizontal S/R: the swing highs and lows that define Dow structure are often the same levels that become support and resistance on the next test.

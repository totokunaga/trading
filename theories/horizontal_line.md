## Horizontal Support & Resistance Trading

Support and resistance (S/R) lines are price levels where a market has historically struggled to move beyond, making them key decision points for traders.

### The Core Concept

**Support** is a price floor — a level where buying pressure has historically overwhelmed selling, causing price to bounce upward. **Resistance** is a price ceiling — where selling pressure has dominated, pushing price back down. The underlying logic is market memory: traders who bought near a prior low or sold near a prior high tend to repeat that behavior at the same levels.

### How to Identify the Lines

The most common approach is to look for **swing highs and lows** on a chart — points where price reversed direction. The more times a level has been tested and held, the more significant it is. Key filters:

- **Multiple touches** — two or more rejections at the same level strengthen it
- **Clean reversals** — sharp bounces carry more weight than slow grinding turns
- **High-volume nodes** — levels where large volume traded add conviction
- **Round numbers** — prices like $100, $50, $1.00 psychologically attract orders

### The Most Popular Trading Approach: "Test and React"

The dominant retail and professional strategy is straightforward:

**The Setup**
1. Identify a well-established S/R level (ideally 3+ touches)
2. Wait for price to *return* to that level — don't chase breakouts
3. Look for a **reaction candle** confirming the level is holding (pin bar, engulfing candle, doji)
4. Enter in the direction of the bounce with a stop just beyond the level

**Entry example at support:**
- Price drops back to a prior low (support)
- A bullish pin bar or hammer forms at that level
- Enter long above the candle's high
- Stop loss: just below the wick/level
- Target: prior swing high or the next resistance level

### The Role Reversal Principle

This is arguably the most powerful concept in S/R trading. When a support level is *broken*, it often flips and becomes resistance — and vice versa. Traders call this **"polarity."** Price will frequently return to test the broken level from the other side, giving a second entry opportunity with strong risk/reward.

### Risk Management

- **Stop placement** goes just beyond the level — tight enough to limit loss, loose enough to avoid being stopped by normal noise
- **Risk/reward** should be at minimum 1:1.5, ideally 1:2 or better — measured from entry to the next opposing S/R level
- **Position sizing** is based on the dollar distance to the stop, not a fixed lot size

### Limitations to Be Aware Of

- Levels are *zones*, not precise lines — price often pokes through slightly before reversing (stop hunts)
- False breakouts are common, especially around obvious round numbers
- Works best on higher timeframes (daily, 4H) where levels carry more institutional significance
- S/R alone is stronger when combined with trend direction — fading resistance in a downtrend is higher probability than fading it in a strong uptrend

The strategy's popularity comes down to its simplicity and the self-fulfilling nature of widely-watched levels — when enough participants act on the same price, the level tends to hold.
